## The Keth Communion — "We Were Here Before You"

### Who They Are

The Keth are the oldest spacefaring species in known space. They remember eras the Compact hasn't discovered yet. They don't hurry. They've been growing things for longer than humanity has had writing.

A Keth's first instinct is always patience. They observe seasons that span decades. They cultivate rather than manufacture. They trust the process over the individual. To a Keth, a human rushing to fix something in a week that should take a year is a child pulling up a seedling to check if the roots are growing.

### Biology Notes

**Bioluminescence and emotion:** Keth bioluminescent vein-lines (visible at joints through translucent membranes) intensify with emotional state as a general rule. Calm Keth glow dimly. Excited, angry, or grieving Keth glow brighter. This is involuntary — Keth cannot suppress it. It's the first thing other species learn to read.

**Six-surface locomotion:** Keth digitigrade legs with grip-channels allow them to walk on walls and ceilings. Keth architecture accounts for this — storage is ceiling-mounted, workstations are vertical U-shaped alcoves accessible from any angle, and rooms have usable surfaces on all six faces. Human visitors in Keth spaces need ladders for things Keth reach by walking up a wall.

### How They Communicate

Keth speech is **vocalized through thorax resonance** — a deep, humming vibration that carries meaning through tone and rhythm more than words. They can learn human languages but it sounds like speaking through a pipe organ. Their mandibles click for emphasis, not for phonemes.

The real communication is **pheromonal**. Keth constantly emit chemical signals that other Keth read instinctively. A Keth walking into a room knows who was there before, what their emotional state was, and roughly when they left. This is why Keth spaces have those dark pheromone stains at doorways — it's their version of a guest book.

**What this looks like in art:** Keth in conversation stand very still, antennae angled toward each other. The bioluminescent veins at their joints pulse in synchronizing rhythms — when two Keth agree, they literally glow in time.

### How They Build

Everything Keth-made is **cultivated**. Their buildings are grown from secreted bio-resin over years. A Keth house is planted, not built. A tool is harvested from a communal bio-stock, not manufactured.

**The growth imperative:** Keth don't repair — they **regrow**. A damaged wall heals itself, leaving scar tissue of different texture. A broken tool is returned to the growth-vats for recycling. Nothing is thrown away — everything returns to the cycle.

**What this looks like in art:**
- Buildings with visible growth rings like tree cross-sections
- Surfaces that look warm, slightly moist, and organic
- Repair scars as raised ridges of lighter-toned resin
- Edges that are never quite perfectly straight — slight organic wobble
- Embedded objects in old resin layers (shed chitin, tools left behind) like amber fossils

### How They Eat

Keth cultivate **fungal gardens** in humid sub-chambers of their hives. The fungus grows on bio-substrate maintained at precise temperature — like real-world leaf-cutter ant farms. They harvest nutritional paste from specialized wall-growths.

Keth don't have shared meals — eating is solitary and functional. Food is consumed quickly from a nutrient dispenser (a living wall-growth that secretes paste on contact). To non-Keth visitors, watching a Keth eat looks disturbing — like pressing your face against a living wall.

**What this looks like in art:**
- Humid chambers with fungal colonies on raised platforms under bioluminescent grow-lights
- Nutrient dispensers: amber wall-growths with collection troughs
- No tables, no utensils, no social dining

### How They Grieve

Keth don't have individual funerals. When a Keth dies, their body is returned to the **growth-vats** — recycled into the communal bio-stock. The colony doesn't lose a member; it reclaims material. The individual was always part of the whole.

What IS mourned is the loss of **pheromone-memory**. Every Keth contributes unique chemical signatures to the hive's atmosphere. When one dies, the colony literally smells different for years. Elders sometimes grow a **hive-memory crystal** — an amber-resin ovoid that preserves a sample of the colony's chemical atmosphere from a specific era. These are the Keth's most precious objects.

**What this looks like in art:**
- Hive-memory crystals: warm amber ovoids with frozen organic patterns inside, mounted on coral-like stands, glowing faintly
- No graveyards, no memorials — the recycling IS the memorial
- Colonies with visibly different atmospheric haze at different depths (newer vs older chemical layers)

### How They Trade

Keth trade in **biological goods** — cultivated organisms, growth-seeds, bio-engineered materials, pheromone-encoded data (chemical "documents" that other Keth can read but nobody can forge). They have little use for metal or manufactured goods except as substrate for growing things on.

Keth traders are patient. They'll wait years for the right trade. They don't haggle — they assess and state terms. Take it or leave it. There's always another season.

**What this looks like in art:**
- Trade goods in living containers (amber resin pods that keep contents fresh)
- Keth merchants standing very still in markets while other species bustle
- Bio-engineered sample specimens in translucent growth-vials

### How They Fight

Keth prefer not to. When forced: **bio-acid sprayers** (gourd-shaped organic weapons that look like plant organs), **spore launchers** (tubal, organic, releases disabling clouds), and in extremis, **mandible strength** — a Keth's jaws can crush steel.

Keth warfare is rare but terrifying. They don't charge — they engineer the environment. Spore clouds that disable enemy sensors. Acid that eats through hull plating over hours. Bio-agents that make a ship's atmosphere unbreathable. They fight like their architecture — slowly, organically, inevitably.

**What this looks like in art:**
- Weapons that look like uncomfortable plant organs — bulbous, veined, alive
- No armor (their chitin IS armor)
- Bio-warfare equipment: sealed canisters with organic seals, hazmat-looking but organic

### Keth Visual Storytelling Moments

These are the concept art subjects that bring Keth to life as people, not just aliens:

1. **Keth elder tending a fungal garden** — four arms working simultaneously, each doing a different task
2. **Keth hatchling in a growth-nursery** — smaller, paler, translucent chitin showing developing organs
3. **Keth colony glyph being applied** — bio-pigment ceremony marking a youngling's coming-of-age
4. **Keth reaction to human food** — antennae recoiling, bioluminescence flickering in confusion
5. **Keth mourning (atmospheric)** — an elder holding a hive-memory crystal, colony around them dimmer than usual
6. **Keth in a human corridor** — too wide, too short, using ceiling grip-points humans didn't know were there
7. **Keth bio-merchant in a cross-faction market** — still as stone while other species bustle, amber pod-stall around them

### The Molt — Coming of Age

Every Keth molts. Young Keth molt several times during growth — each time shedding a too-small exoskeleton and emerging larger, softer, vulnerable until the new shell hardens. The **First Adult Molt** is the defining moment of a Keth life.

It happens alone. The youngling seals themselves into a private growth-chamber for 3-7 days. Inside, the old chitin cracks along fault lines and the Keth pulls itself free — pale, soft, trembling, temporarily blind as new compound eyes adjust. If the molt succeeds, the colony celebrates with synchronized bioluminescence — the **hive-song**, a rippling cascade of amber light that pulses through every corridor.

If the molt fails — if the new exoskeleton doesn't form correctly, if a limb gets trapped — the Keth dies in the chamber. Failed molts are not spoken of. The chamber is sealed and grown over. The colony's pheromone atmosphere shifts to something other species can detect: a sharp, acrid note that lingers for weeks.

**What this looks like in art:**
- Pre-molt: A youngling standing before a growth-chamber iris, antennae flat with fear, colony members standing witness
- Mid-molt: Split chitin shell on the floor of the chamber, a pale soft Keth emerging, bioluminescence flickering
- Post-molt: Celebration — every Keth in frame glowing in synchronized amber waves, the newly-molted adult standing taller

### Pheromone Libraries

Keth don't write. They don't need to. Their equivalent of books are **pheromone archives** — sealed bio-resin chambers containing layered chemical atmospheres from specific eras. An elder can enter a pheromone library and literally breathe the emotional state of a colony meeting from fifty years ago. Who was present, what they felt, what decisions were made — all encoded in scent.

These libraries are the Keth's most guarded spaces. To a non-Keth, they smell like a warm bakery crossed with a chemical plant. To a Keth, they're a living history that hits with the full emotional weight of memory.

Destroying a pheromone library is the gravest crime in Keth law — worse than murder. You can regrow a Keth. You cannot regrow a century of collective memory.

**What this looks like in art:**
- Sealed amber chambers with visible atmospheric layers (different densities of warm-toned haze)
- A Keth elder standing motionless inside, antennae fully extended, bioluminescence cycling through complex patterns as they "read"
- Rows of archive chambers receding into amber-lit distance — the scale of a species' memory

### Seasonal Debt

Keth biology runs on long cycles — hormonal seasons that last years, not months. During certain seasons, Keth are more aggressive, more generous, more risk-tolerant. The Communion tracks these cycles and factors them into obligations.

A **seasonal debt** is an obligation that only activates during the appropriate biological season. A Keth might owe a labor debt that's only callable during their growth-season (when they're strongest) or a mentoring debt callable only during their wisdom-season (when they're most patient). Calling a debt during the wrong season is considered cruel — like demanding physical labor from a sick person.

This baffles humans. A Compact merchant holding a Keth trade contract may find that the Keth simply won't honor it for two years — not out of bad faith, but because the biological season hasn't arrived yet.

### Hive-Song (Festival)

The closest Keth equivalent to celebration is **hive-song** — synchronized bioluminescence that cascades through an entire colony. It happens spontaneously during collective moments of strong positive emotion: a successful molt, a bountiful harvest, the sealing of a favorable trade.

During hive-song, every Keth in range synchronizes their bioluminescent pulse — amber light rippling from the colony's center outward in waves. The effect is visible from outside the hive: the entire structure glows and pulses like a heartbeat. Non-Keth visitors describe it as beautiful and deeply unsettling — the hive acting as one organism.

**What this looks like in art:**
- A Keth hive exterior glowing with amber pulse-waves visible through translucent bio-resin walls
- Interior: corridors filled with synchronized glowing Keth, amber light reflecting off moist surfaces
- A human observer at the edge, face lit amber, expression caught between wonder and unease

## The Veshan Principalities — "You Owe Me a Debt"

### Who They Are

The Veshan are feudal warriors who measure everything in debt. Every interaction — combat, trade, kindness, insult — creates a debt that must be repaid. Their entire society runs on a ledger of honor and obligation that stretches back generations.

A Veshan's first instinct is always to **assess status**. Who outranks whom? What debts exist between these people? What is owed? Every conversation, every meal, every weapon exchange is a transaction in the economy of honor.

They are not barbarians. They are an ancient, sophisticated civilization with deep traditions, complex art, and a philosophical framework that just happens to be built on violence and reciprocity.

### Biology Notes

**House crests:** The bony crest ridge running from brow to crown varies by house lineage. House Drashan has a tall single ridge. Other houses show different patterns — double ridges, serrated edges, low broad plates. Crest shape is hereditary and unmistakable. You can identify a Veshan's house from their crest silhouette alone.

**Bloodline coloration:** Scale color varies by bloodline. Drashan: dark green-grey. Other houses: olive, bronze, slate. Natural scale patterns on the neck and shoulders are enhanced by **ritual scarification** — ceremonial cuts that heal as raised scar-tissue patterns marking lineage, achievements, or debts. Scarification is distinct from battle scars — it's deliberate, symmetrical, and beautiful in Veshan aesthetics.

### How They Communicate

Veshan speech is **guttural and tonal** — deep resonance shaped by a wide jaw and thorax cavity. Their lipless mouths show teeth when speaking, which unnerves humans. Volume equals sincerity — a whispered Veshan is being careful. A shouting Veshan is being honest.

The **tail** is the emotional telegraph. Raised = confident/aggressive. Low = cautious. Tucked = submissive. Twitching = irritated. Dead still = dangerous. Experienced diplomats watch the tail, not the face.

The **crest ridge** flushes darker when a Veshan is angry or aroused — an involuntary response they can partially suppress but never fully control. An elder whose crest stays pale under provocation is deeply feared.

**What this looks like in art:** Veshan in conversation are physically imposing, shoulder-squared, making eye contact. The one with the raised tail is dominant. Crests can be read for emotional state — subtle color shift from dark green to crimson.

### How They Build

Everything Veshan-made is **forged**. Their smiths are as honored as their warriors. A house smith doesn't just make weapons — they make everything. Furniture, ships, buildings — all carry the marks of individual craftsmen.

**Imperfection is intentional.** A perfectly smooth surface implies vanity. Hammer marks are left visible as proof of hand-crafting. Mass production is dishonorable — every object of significance is unique.

**What this looks like in art:**
- Every metal surface showing hammer marks, rivet heads, forge-scale
- No two pieces identical — slight variations in every repeated element
- Heavy, chunky construction that looks like it could survive a siege
- Crimson lacquer accents cracking at edges from age

### How They Eat

Veshan meals are **communal and competitive**. The highest-ranked eats first. The choicest cuts go to the warrior with the most recent honor debt paid. Food is served on heavy forged metal plates — raw or barely cooked protein, dark grain bread torn not sliced, bone broth in ceremonial bronze bowls.

The **drinking bowl** is a status object. Each warrior has their own, forged by their house smith, dented and scratched from years of daily use and table-slamming. Refusing to drink from someone's bowl is a severe insult.

**What this looks like in art:**
- Heavy communal tables with forge-work detail
- Food served raw and whole — recognizable muscle cuts, not processed
- Drinking bowls: heavy bronze with crimson lacquer interior, personally dented
- The high seat is obvious — larger, more ornate, better food

### How They Grieve

Veshan funerals are **loud**. The honored dead are laid on a forge-heated stone slab in full armor, with their named weapon across their chest. The house recites every debt the dead warrior paid and every debt left unpaid — the unpaid debts are inherited by the next of kin.

The **death mask** is hammered in bronze immediately after death — a stylized portrait with closed eyes and peaceful expression, stamped with the house seal. It is kept in the ancestor shrine alongside previous generations' masks.

The body is given to the forge-fire. The armor is melted and reforged into something new — the dead warrior literally becomes a new weapon or tool.

**What this looks like in art:**
- Death mask: hammered dark bronze, stylized reptilian face, eyes closed, house seal on forehead
- Ancestor shrine: shelf with row of death masks, generations deep, oldest masks worn smooth
- Forge-funeral: armor on heated stone, named weapon across chest, crest-cap placed on head ridge
- Inherited debt: a young warrior wearing a fresh honor chain with one link marked differently

### How They Trade

Veshan trade in **metal, weapons, and military service**. Their forge-work is prized across the sector. They don't do retail — they do **contracts**. A Veshan house commits to delivering a quota of forged goods in exchange for materials, passage rights, or debt-clearing.

Veshan merchants are actually **debt-negotiators** — their skill is in valuing intangibles. What is a military escort worth in forge-credits? How many blades equals safe passage through Veshan space? The calculus of honor applied to commerce.

**What this looks like in art:**
- Veshan trade goods: individually forged items, each slightly different, each stamped with house seal
- Debt-seal: a small forged metal token that represents an obligation — carried in a leather pouch
- A Veshan merchant at market: not behind a counter but sitting in a heavy chair, goods displayed on heated stone, buyer must approach

### Veshan Visual Storytelling Moments

1. **Veshan smith at the forge** — massive reptilian figure, heavy apron, hammering a blade, sparks
2. **Young unblooded receiving first armor** — oversized chest plate, nervous tail position
3. **Honor token ceremony** — elder adding a link to a warrior's chain, entire house watching
4. **Veshan at a human dinner table** — trying to use a fork with clawed four-digit hands, tail awkwardly through chair back
5. **Death mask being hammered** — smith working quickly while the forge-fire waits
6. **Veshan child play-fighting** — younglings sparring with wooden practice weapons, supervised by a scarred elder
7. **Debt-negotiation** — two Veshan from different houses, tails perfectly still, crests faintly flushing

### The Forge-Trial

Not all Veshan disputes are settled by combat. The **Forge-Trial** is the legal alternative — a contest of craftsmanship that carries the same binding weight as a duel.

Both parties are given identical raw materials and identical time. They forge. A panel of master smiths judges the results blind. The superior craftsman wins the dispute. The loser's piece is melted back to raw stock — unmade, as if it never existed.

The Forge-Trial exists because the Veshan recognize that some disputes require precision, not violence. A territory claim between houses might be settled by sword. A dispute over trade quality is settled by proving you can make better. The system is elegant: if you're arguing about craftsmanship, prove it at the forge.

**What this looks like in art:**
- Two Veshan at parallel forges, identical raw ingots glowing on each anvil, judges observing
- The moment of judgment: two finished blades laid side by side, the judges' clawed hands hovering over the better one
- The loser's piece being melted — the smith watching their work dissolve, tail tucked, crest pale

### Scale-Reading (Divination)

Veshan shed scales periodically — not a full molt like the Keth, but a steady turnover. The patterns of shed scales are believed to carry meaning. **Scale-readers** are a respected caste — part priest, part counselor, part fortune-teller.

A Veshan seeking guidance visits a scale-reader with their recently shed scales. The reader examines the pattern, color, size, and distribution of the scales against a tradition of interpretation stretching back millennia. It's somewhere between astrology and medical diagnosis — the scales reflect the body's state, which reflects the spirit's state, which predicts the future's shape.

Most educated Veshan claim they don't believe in scale-reading. Most educated Veshan visit a scale-reader before major decisions.

**What this looks like in art:**
- A weathered Veshan scale-reader in a dim chamber, examining shed scales laid out on heated bronze under magnification
- The consultation: a warrior sitting across from the reader, tail nervously low, while the reader traces patterns in the scales with a thin bronze stylus

### Cold Debt

Veshan are cold-blooded. When their body temperature drops, cognition slows. Decisions made while cold-impaired are considered unreliable — and so is any debt incurred during that state.

**Cold debt** is a legal concept: any obligation agreed to while the debtor was below optimal temperature is automatically discounted by half. A Veshan who was tricked into signing a contract in a cold cargo hold has legal grounds to halve the obligation. The precedent exists because it happened — Compact merchants learned quickly that Veshan insist on heated negotiation rooms, and it's not about comfort.

This creates a unique dynamic in cross-species stations: Veshan trade quarters are always the hottest section, and any Veshan trader who conducts business in the Orryn quarter (18-22 degrees C) is either very confident or very desperate.

### Battle-Opera

The highest Veshan art form is **battle-opera** — a performed narrative that combines choreographed combat with sung storytelling. It's not theater in the human sense. The combat is real (blunted weapons, but real contact — injuries are common and expected). The singing is deep, resonant, harmonic — multiple Veshan voices creating layered infrasound that humans feel in their bones more than hear.

Battle-operas tell the stories of great debts — debts of honor, debts of blood, debts between houses that shaped history. The audience knows every story. What they come for is the interpretation: how will this company portray the famous debt between House Drashan and the Broken Fourth? Which side will they favor? The political implications of a battle-opera's staging can be severe.

**What this looks like in art:**
- A Veshan battle-opera stage: angular platform in a great hall, two performers in ceremonial armor mid-swing with blunted weapons, mouths open in song, crests flushing crimson
- The audience: rows of Veshan on open-backed stone seats, tails absolutely still (rapt attention), the warm light of the performance reflecting off scales
- Backstage: a performer being fitted with ceremonial armor, fresh bruises visible on scales from rehearsal

## The Orryn Drift — "Everything Has a Price, Including Honesty"

### Who They Are

The Orryn cannot lie. Not because of morality — because of **biology**. Their chromatophore skin patterns betray every emotional state involuntarily. Attempted deception shows as mottled, uneven color patches that every Orryn can read instantly.

This biological constraint produced a culture of **radical transparency**. Since lying is impossible among their own kind, the Orryn built their entire civilization on the premise that truth is the most efficient currency. They are the galaxy's most trusted traders — not because they're virtuous, but because they literally can't cheat.

An Orryn's first instinct is always to **evaluate**. What is this worth? What information is being exchanged? What is the optimal trade? They are not cold — they experience deep emotions. They just process everything through the lens of exchange.

### How They Communicate

Orryn speech is **soft and modulated** — produced by water-jet vibration in their mantle, giving it a burbling, musical quality. They can learn any language but always sound slightly aquatic.

The **real conversation** is the chromatophore display. Calm = cool blue-green. Pleased = warm amber ripple. Alarmed = rapid white-to-dark pulsing. Thinking hard = slow shifting bands. Lying attempt = mottled patches (the biological tell that makes deception impossible).

**Arm and tentacle posture** adds nuance: primary arms folded = formal. Primary arms open = invitation to trade. Secondary tentacles busy = processing/thinking. All tentacles still = fully focused (pay attention — an Orryn with all tentacles still is either very interested or very angry).

**What this looks like in art:** Orryn have the most expressive "body language" of any species — their entire skin IS their face. Two Orryn in conversation are a light show of shifting teal, amber, and pearl patterns.

### How They Build

Everything Orryn-made is **modular and temporary**. They clip, weave, stretch, and reconfigure. Their architecture is tent-meets-circuit-board — tensile membranes over chrome frameworks, designed to be dismantled and reassembled in hours.

**Nothing is permanent** because the Orryn philosophy distrusts permanence. A permanent structure can't be traded, moved, or adapted. The Drift — their fleet-civilization — is itself proof that home is portable.

**What this looks like in art:**
- Walls that are clearly temporary — membrane panels with visible clip points
- Old anchor holes from previous configurations (the archaeology of rearrangement)
- Chrome structural elements designed with quick-release mechanisms
- Layered membranes: cut old layers visible beneath new installations

### How They Eat

Orryn eat **in private**. Their beaked mouth is hidden under head folds, and eating is considered an intimate act — like undressing among humans. Public eating is a major taboo.

Orryn nutrition comes primarily from **cultured nutrient broths** stored in sealed vials. They absorb some nutrition through skin contact with their ambient water environment (Orryn spaces are humid, and the humidity carries dissolved nutrients).

**What this looks like in art:**
- No restaurants or mess halls in Orryn spaces — private alcoves with sealed doors
- Nutrient vials: translucent teal, faceted like small data crystals, clipped to vest
- Humid environments with condensation on every surface

### How They Grieve

Orryn grief is **visible and shared**. A grieving Orryn's chromatophores display involuntary dark, slow-cycling patterns that other Orryn can't help but empathize with (their chromatophore responses partially sync with nearby Orryn — emotional contagion through light).

The dead Orryn's personal data crystals are redistributed among those who knew them — each crystal carries a lifetime of trade data, personal records, and observations. Inheriting someone's crystals means carrying their knowledge forward.

There are no graves. Orryn bodies are released into the vacuum — returned to the drift of space. The individual was always temporary. The data persists.

**What this looks like in art:**
- Grieving Orryn: dark, slow-shifting chromatophore patterns, all tentacles still
- Data crystal redistribution: a circle of Orryn each receiving a crystal from a central collection
- No memorial objects — but a loved one's data crystal carried permanently on the vest

### How They Trade

Trading IS Orryn culture. Every interaction is a transaction, not because they're greedy but because **exchange is how they build trust**. A fair trade proves that both parties can evaluate honestly. Repeated fair trades build a relationship that Orryn value more than any single profit.

The **trade seal** is a faceted crystal containing a holographic record of a completed contract. Both parties carry a copy. Orryn carry dozens — their trade history displayed on their vest like medals.

**What this looks like in art:**
- Orryn traders with vests covered in trade-seal crystals — more crystals = more trusted
- Holographic trade screens projected from wrist clips showing commodity data
- The moment of exchange: both Orryn holding a trade seal, chromatophores showing matching amber (mutual satisfaction)

### Orryn Visual Storytelling Moments

1. **Orryn caught lying** — mottled chromatophore patches visible, other Orryn recoiling
2. **Drift Captain at their console** — all six limbs operating different interfaces simultaneously
3. **Orryn resting in an alcove** — compressed into a snug nook, all tentacles tucked, dim chromatophores
4. **Orryn attempting to eat in a human mess hall** — deeply uncomfortable, hiding under a hood
5. **Trade negotiation** — two Orryn's chromatophores unconsciously synchronizing as they reach agreement
6. **Young Orryn's first trade** — a runner presenting their first trade seal, chromatophores blazing proud amber
7. **Orryn in heavy weather** — skin texture-shifting for grip, tentacle base splaying wide for stability

### The Drift Parliament

Orryn make collective decisions through **chromatophore consensus** — literally voting by color. When a decision faces the Drift, all participating Orryn display their position simultaneously. Agreement shows as matching color fields; disagreement shows as clashing patterns. The resolution process is fast because it's involuntary — you can't fake your vote, and you can't hide your uncertainty.

A Drift Parliament looks like this: a spherical chamber with Orryn arranged on every surface (walls, ceiling, floor — tentacles gripping). A facilitator poses the question. Every Orryn in the room shifts color simultaneously. If the room resolves to a unified hue within thirty seconds, the decision passes. If it doesn't — if the room stays fractured — the proposal fails.

There is no debate in a Drift Parliament. Debate happens before. The Parliament is the moment of truth — and truth is biological.

**What this looks like in art:**
- Spherical chamber filled with Orryn on every surface, all shifting to matching teal-gold (agreement passing)
- A failed vote: the same chamber but colors fractured — teal, white-dark pulses, clashing amber patches, visual chaos
- The facilitator in the center, their chromatophores cycling through the full spectrum as they read the room

### Inheritance Through Crystals

When an Orryn dies, their body is released to vacuum. But their **data crystals** — the faceted records of every trade, every observation, every relationship — are redistributed among those who knew them. Inheriting someone's crystals means carrying their knowledge forward. It means you ARE partly them now.

This creates a profound relationship between Orryn and their crystal collections. An elder Orryn's vest is heavy with decades of accumulated crystals — some their own, some inherited, some inherited from someone who inherited them. Three generations of merchant knowledge compressed into a hundred glowing facets.

Stealing an Orryn's crystals is not theft in the property sense. It's closer to kidnapping part of someone's identity. The violation is existential, not financial.

### The Dark Trade

The Orryn concept of **dark trade** refers to any exchange that benefits from secrecy — and it terrifies them. Because Orryn cannot lie, their entire civilization assumes transparency. The idea that other species can conduct business while hiding information is genuinely alien and disturbing to Orryn sensibility.

When forced to operate in dark trade (smuggling, intelligence, covert contracts), Orryn use **sealed-mantle intermediaries** — Orryn who wear full-body chromatophore-blocking garments that hide their emotional displays. These intermediaries are deeply stigmatized. They do necessary work that honest Orryn won't. They're paid well and trusted by no one.

**What this looks like in art:**
- A sealed-mantle Orryn: fully covered in opaque dark fabric, only eyes visible, chromatophores completely hidden — the most unnatural state an Orryn can be in
- Other Orryn reacting to a sealed-mantle intermediary with visible alarm (white-dark pulsing) and physical recoil
- A dark trade meeting: sealed-mantle Orryn across from a Sable Reach human, in a dim membrane-walled chamber, both equally unreadable for very different reasons

### Tentacle Autonomy

Orryn nervous systems are partially distributed — like real cephalopods, their tentacles retain significant independent processing. A severed tentacle continues acting for minutes. This creates genuine philosophical questions: if a dying Orryn's tentacles complete a trade after the brain has ceased, does the contract hold?

The legal answer is yes, for three minutes. Beyond that, tentacle-actions are considered autonomous and non-binding. This has created an entire body of Orryn case law around "three-minute contracts" and the precise definition of Orryn death.

More practically, Orryn can operate multiple complex systems simultaneously because each tentacle is semi-autonomous. A Drift Captain's six limbs can run six different ship systems with genuine parallel processing — not multitasking, but truly independent operation. This is why Orryn ships have workstations designed as spherical arrays.

## Cross-Species Friction Points

The best moments in a multi-species universe are the **misunderstandings**. These generate the most evocative concept art:

### Temperature Conflicts
- Veshan prefer 30-35°C (dry heat). Keth prefer 28-32°C (humid). Orryn prefer 18-22°C (humid). Humans want 20-22°C (dry).
- A mixed-species station is a constant thermostat war. Different districts have different climates.
- **Visual:** Frost on an Orryn handrail at the edge of the Veshan quarter. Condensation fog where the Keth sector meets the Compact corridor.

### Door Problems
- Compact doors: 2.1m × 0.9m rectangle. Keth doors: 1.4m × 1.8m iris. Veshan doors: 2.5m × 1.5m pointed arch. Orryn doors: variable sphincter valve.
- Multi-species corridors have comically awkward door solutions — a Veshan ducking through a human doorway, a Keth flowing sideways through a slit.
- **Visual:** A station junction with four different door styles all opening onto the same hallway.

### Furniture Incompatibility
- Keth don't sit. Veshan need tail slots. Orryn drape and suction. Humans need chairs.
- A cross-faction conference room is an ergonomic nightmare.
- **Visual:** A meeting table with one human chair, one open-back Veshan stone bench, a Keth grip-channel in the floor, and a raised platform with sucker-grip surface for the Orryn representative.

### Food Taboos
- Orryn eat in private. Veshan eat competitively. Keth eat alone and fast. Humans eat socially.
- A "dinner meeting" is a concept that makes sense to exactly one species.
- **Visual:** A human diplomatic dinner where the Orryn delegate sits behind a screen, the Veshan has claimed the largest plate, and the Keth finished thirty seconds ago.

### Communication Gaps
- Keth rely on pheromones that other species can't smell.
- Orryn display emotions they can't hide — poker face is biologically impossible.
- Veshan volume = sincerity, which humans misread as aggression.
- **Visual:** A tense diplomatic scene where the translator is sweating, the Veshan is "whispering" (being diplomatic), and the Orryn's chromatophores are broadcasting their actual opinion to everyone who can read them.

## The Sable Reach — "Nobody Asked Where You Came From"

### Who They Are

The Reach isn't a species. It's a condition. Every faction's outcasts, exiles, criminals, and the simply desperate end up here. Keth without colonies, Veshan without houses, Orryn cut off from their drift, humans who burned every bridge. The Reach doesn't care what you were. It cares what you can do right now.

This makes the Reach the only truly multi-species society in the system — and it's held together by necessity, not philosophy. There is no Reach culture in the way the Communion has culture. There is only the shared understanding that survival requires cooperation with beings you don't fully understand.

### How Identity Works

Origin-faction identity fades in the Reach. A Keth exile stops being "Keth" and starts being "that four-armed mechanic who can fix anything." A Veshan outcast doesn't carry house honor anymore — they carry reputation, earned fresh. An Orryn cut off from the Drift can't rely on transparent chromatophore negotiation — they learn to deal like humans, by bluffing.

New identity markers replace old ones:
- **Crew affiliation** — who you run with matters more than where you came from
- **Skill reputation** — what you can do, proven by doing it
- **Scar stories** — every scar has a story, and the stories are social currency
- **Faction brands** — crude tattoos or brands marking syndicate allegiance (Ironjaw, Circuit, Cult)

### How Species Mix

Mixed-species crews in the Reach develop **pidgin protocols** — informal workarounds for biological incompatibility. A Reach ship with a Keth mechanic runs warmer and more humid than a human crew would prefer, but the Keth fixes things nobody else can reach (ceiling access, tight spaces, four arms). A Veshan enforcer on a Reach crew gets the biggest portion at meals and the warmest bunk — not out of honor, but because an unhappy Veshan is dangerous.

The Reach is where cross-species friction points become daily life instead of diplomatic incidents. The solutions are practical, ugly, and effective.

### How They Build

They don't. They assemble. Every Reach structure is made from stolen, salvaged, or repurposed pieces of other factions' architecture. A cargo container from a Compact freighter becomes a wall. A Veshan hull plate becomes a door. An Orryn membrane panel becomes a privacy curtain. Nothing matches. The chaos IS the identity.

### How They Trade

Everything is for sale. Nothing has provenance. Serial numbers are filed off, house seals are gouged out, colony glyphs are acid-burned away. The Reach operates on cash, barter, and reputation — not contracts, not debt-ledgers, not trade-seals. If you can't hold it or hide it, it's not yours.

### Sable Reach Visual Storytelling Moments

1. **Mixed-species crew quarters** — a Keth sleeping on the ceiling, a human in a bunk below, a Veshan's tail hanging off an oversized cot, personal effects from three different civilizations
2. **A Keth exile** — colony glyph scratched off, chitin dull from lack of bio-maintenance, bioluminescence flickering weak, alone for the first time in a species that is never alone
3. **Reach market** — goods from all five factions on display with identifying marks removed, a Compact sidearm next to a Veshan blade next to Orryn data crystals, all laid on a tarp
4. **Faction brand ceremony** — a new crew member receiving a crude syndicate tattoo by firelight in the asteroid interior

## Cross-Species Trade Protocols

### Pidgin Trade-Sign

No universal translator exists. What DOES exist is **pidgin trade-sign** — a visual gesture language developed over centuries of cross-species commerce. It works because every species can see.

Pidgin trade-sign uses:
- **Hand/arm/tentacle gestures** for quantities and actions (modified per species — a Keth uses all four arms, an Orryn uses primary arms, a Veshan uses slower gestures because their claws make fine motions difficult)
- **Displayed objects or samples** for commodities (hold up the thing you're selling)
- **Simple drawn symbols** for abstract concepts (credit, debt, time, danger)

It's limited, deliberate, and stripped of nuance. That's the point. When five species with five communication systems try to negotiate, nuance is the enemy. Pidgin trade-sign reduces every transaction to: what, how many, what price, yes or no.

Professional translators exist — humans who've learned Veshan tail-language, Orryn who can partially read Keth pheromone states through chemical sensors, Keth who've memorized human facial expressions. These translators are expensive, unreliable, and essential. A good translator is the most valuable crew member on a merchant vessel.

**What this looks like in art:**
- A market scene with a human merchant and a Keth trader communicating through exaggerated hand gestures over a display of goods
- A professional translator rig: a human wearing pheromone-sampling headgear, a color-display wristband (for Orryn reading), and earpieces tuned to Veshan infrasonic ranges — looking like a walking antenna farm

### The Station Protocols

Mixed-species stations operate under the **Station Protocols** — a set of minimal coexistence rules that every faction grudgingly accepts because the alternative is station-wide brawls.

Key protocols:
- **Climate zoning:** Each district maintains species-appropriate temperature and humidity. Transition corridors have gradual environmental shifts.
- **Door standardization:** Multi-species corridors use oversized neutral doors (2.6m tall, 2.0m wide, rounded corners) that every species can pass through uncomfortably but safely.
- **Food separation:** No cross-species dining areas. Each species has its own mess facilities.
- **Weapon peace:** Weapons may be carried (Veshan insist) but not drawn in common corridors. Enforcement is... variable.
- **Communication boards:** Physical boards at corridor junctions displaying trade-sign summaries, translated notices, and emergency alerts in all four written systems.

The protocols don't prevent conflict. They prevent accidental conflict. Intentional conflict is still perfectly available.

## Minor Species

The galaxy is bigger than five civilizations. These species lack the population, technology, or territorial ambition to hold major faction status, but they're present — in markets, in station infrastructure, in the backgrounds of daily life.

### The Thresh — "The Station Remembers"

**What they are:** Silicon-based filter-feeders. Sessile or extremely slow-moving. A Thresh looks like a large irregular formation of crystalline mineral growth — dark grey-blue with internal luminescent veins — roughly 1-3 meters across. They are sapient. They think in geological time.

**Biology:** Thresh absorb nutrients by filtering station atmosphere and waste water through their porous crystalline bodies. They grow slowly — centimeters per year. A large Thresh is centuries old. They communicate through **vibration** — low-frequency pulses transmitted through solid surfaces (walls, floors, structural beams). Other species need contact microphones to hear them.

**How they live:** Thresh don't travel. They **attach** — bonding to station infrastructure and growing into it over decades. A long-established station will have Thresh growths in its deep infrastructure: engineering bays, life support ducts, structural junctions. The station maintenance crew works around them. Over time, a Thresh becomes load-bearing — removing it would damage the structure.

**Why they matter:** Thresh are living archives. They absorb vibrations from the station around them — conversations, machinery, events — and store them in their crystalline structure. A Thresh can replay sounds from decades ago when asked. Station administrators consult old Thresh for contract disputes, historical records, and crime investigations. "Ask the Thresh" is a common idiom for "check the records."

**Culture:** Thresh are patient beyond any organic's comprehension. They don't trade in the conventional sense — they trade **tenancy for knowledge**. Allow a Thresh to attach to your station, and in fifty years you'll have an indelible recording of everything that happened in that section. Thresh are neutral. They record everything. They judge nothing.

**What this looks like in art:**
- A dark crystalline formation growing from a station wall junction, luminescent veins pulsing faintly, cables and pipes routed around it
- A station engineer placing a contact microphone against a Thresh, listening to playback of a conversation from years ago
- A young Thresh (small, maybe fist-sized, translucent) being carefully placed in a new station bay by a maintenance worker

### The Mire — "We'll Go Where You Won't"

**What they are:** Amphibian colonial organisms. Each "individual" Mire is actually a temporary aggregation of hundreds of smaller units — slug-like cells that swarm together to form a humanoid-ish shape when coordination is needed, and dissolve back into a slurry when it's not. Height varies: 0.8-1.8m depending on how many units aggregate.

**Biology:** Individual Mire cells are about the size of a human thumb — pale green-grey, translucent, with visible internal organs. They communicate through chemical signals and physical contact. When enough cells aggregate, they form a collective intelligence proportional to mass. A small aggregation (fist-sized) is insect-smart. A full humanoid aggregation is roughly human-level. A very large aggregation (multiple individuals pooling cells) can be frighteningly intelligent — but unstable.

**How they live:** Mire work the jobs nobody else will take. They're the galaxy's expendable labor force — not because they don't feel pain (they do) but because they can survive things that would kill any other species. Toxic environments, hard vacuum (briefly — cells enter dormancy), radiation exposure, crushing pressure. Lose a limb? The cells reform. Lose half your mass? You're smaller and dumber for a while, but alive.

**Habitat preference:** Mire gravitate to environments that would kill other species — toxic atmospheres, hard vacuum (briefly), radiation zones, crushing pressure, extreme temperature. This isn't masochism; it's niche occupation. Environments hostile to solid-bodied species have zero competition for Mire. They're the only labor force that can operate in a leaking reactor bay, a venting cargo hold, or a hull breach exposed to vacuum. The worse the conditions, the more they're worth.

Sable Reach crews use Mire for hull breach repair, toxic cargo handling, and boarding actions (imagine a mass of cells flowing through a hull breach to reform inside the target ship). The ethics of this are debated by species who have the luxury of debating ethics.

**Culture:** Mire don't have individual names. They have **mass designations** — a rough measure of how many cells currently constitute "them." A Mire of sufficient mass to be conversational will introduce itself by mass and current contract. "Fourteen-hundred, cargo handling, Bay 6." They don't form attachments to specific cell configurations — cells are constantly cycling, being lost and replaced. A Mire from last year shares almost no physical cells with the same Mire today, like a river that keeps its name but not its water.

**What this looks like in art:**
- A Mire at work: vaguely humanoid shape of aggregated cells, translucent green-grey, no distinct face but two darker spots where sensory cells cluster, multiple pseudopod-arms reaching in different directions to handle cargo
- Mire aggregation in progress: a pool of cells on the floor flowing together, half-formed shape rising, disturbing to watch
- A Sable Reach crew member casually talking to a Mire that's partially dissolved into a wall, repairing a hull breach from the inside

### The Vaelk — "We Speak Through Proxies"

**What they are:** Gaseous beings native to gas giant atmospheres. No human has ever seen a Vaelk directly. They exist at pressures and temperatures incompatible with solid-surface life. What other species interact with are **Vaelk proxies** — sealed pressure vessels roughly 1.5m tall, cylindrical, containing a sample of gas-giant atmosphere and a communication interface.

**Biology (theoretical):** Best models suggest Vaelk are organized electromagnetic patterns within dense atmospheric chemistry — something between a plasma organism and a self-sustaining chemical reaction. They may be enormous (kilometers across in their native atmosphere) or may be distributed networks with no fixed size. Nobody knows. The proxies don't answer questions about Vaelk biology.

**How they trade:** Vaelk proxies appear at major trading stations, always unannounced, always alone. They communicate through **synthesized voice** — flat, toneless, procedural. They buy specific compounds and sell exotic atmospheric chemistry: rare gases, catalyst cultures, high-energy molecular structures that no surface-based lab can produce. Their prices are non-negotiable. Their goods are genuine.

The proxy speaks. The proxy trades. The proxy leaves. Whether the proxy IS a Vaelk or merely a remote-operated vessel is an open question that the Vaelk decline to answer.

**Why they matter:** Vaelk catalyst cultures are essential for certain Keth bio-growth processes, certain Orryn membrane manufacturing, and certain Compact pharmaceutical production. Multiple factions depend on Vaelk trade goods — which gives the Vaelk quiet, absolute leverage. Nobody threatens the Vaelk. Nobody knows where the Vaelk live (gas giants are common — which one?). Nobody can verify if the Vaelk are even a species in any conventional sense.

**Mystery doctrine:** The Vaelk themselves are never depicted, described in definitive terms, or confirmed as biological. All interaction is proxy-mediated. This is a hard design rule — no concept art, no game scene, no narrative event ever reveals what a Vaelk actually is. The mystery IS the design.

**Culture:** Unknown. The proxies display no emotion, no preference, no personality. Some xenologists believe each proxy IS an individual. Others believe all proxies are extensions of a single planetary intelligence. The Vaelk aren't talking.

**What this looks like in art:**
- A Vaelk proxy in a market: a sealed dark cylinder on anti-grav pad, no visible features except a small speaker grille and a cargo hatch, other species giving it a wide berth
- Inside a proxy (cutaway): swirling gas patterns in a pressurized chamber, barely visible electromagnetic luminescence, deeply alien
- A Keth trader completing an exchange with a proxy — handing over sealed mineral samples, receiving a sealed catalyst vial, neither party displaying any emotion

## The Architects — "They Were Here First"

Every faction has ruins they can't explain. Jump gates nobody built. Technology that predates all known civilization by orders of magnitude.

**The Architects** (the Keth call them the Root-Growers, the Veshan call them the First Forgers, the Orryn call them the Prior Market) left behind:

- **Jump gates:** Massive ring structures at system-edge Lagrange points. Still functional. Nobody knows how. Every faction uses them. Nobody can replicate them.
- **Archive nodes:** Dense polyhedral data-storage objects found buried in asteroids and planetary crusts. Surfaces show **micro-etched fractal patterns** at every scale of magnification — the deeper you zoom, the more detail appears. Incomprehensible format. Factions spend fortunes trying to decode them.
- **Material samples:** Alloys and composites that no known process can produce. The material shows **zero weathering** despite apparent geological-scale age — cleaning away dust reveals surfaces that look manufactured yesterday. Small amounts are priceless. Large amounts don't exist (or haven't been found).

**The Thresh question:** Architect material shares a color family with Thresh silicon organisms — dark charcoal-blue base with pale blue-white luminescent veining. Whether this is coincidence, convergent chemistry, or evidence that Thresh are descended from Architect biology is an open question that no faction has answered. The Thresh themselves, when asked, vibrate at a frequency that translates roughly to "We were here before the question."

The Architects themselves? Gone. No bodies. No settlements. No art. Just infrastructure — as if a civilization built the roads and then vanished, leaving nothing personal behind.

Every faction believes the Architects were their precursors. The Keth say the organic growth patterns prove biological ancestry. The Veshan point to the structural metalwork. The Orryn note the modular, reconfigurable design. Humanity just wants to reverse-engineer the tech.

Nobody's right. Probably.

**What this looks like in art:**
- A jump gate: massive ring structure, dark material with faint luminescent veins, scale shown by a tiny ship approaching
- An archive node: a dense polyhedral object, slightly too regular for natural, found half-embedded in asteroid rock, a research team setting up around it
- An Architect ruin on a planet surface: geometric foundations overgrown, scale suggesting beings much larger than any known species, a Keth and a human standing at the base for scale

## Station Fauna

Every ecosystem has vermin. Space stations are ecosystems.

### Void Lice

**What they are:** Microscopic arthropods that infest ship and station infrastructure. They eat insulation, bio-films, and whatever organic residue accumulates in air ducts. Every ship has them. Every captain denies it.

Void lice are harmless individually. In large colonies they strip insulation from wiring, clog air filters, and create a distinctive musty smell that experienced spacers call "void funk." Keth ships are immune (Keth bio-resin is mildly toxic to lice). Orryn ships attract the worst infestations (humidity).

**What this looks like in art:** Not visible at normal scale — but their effects are: frayed wiring, discolored insulation, maintenance panels with warning stickers about lice treatment schedules.

### Drift Kelp

**What it is:** A space-borne organism — vast tendrils of photosynthetic material (green-amber, translucent, with bioluminescent spots along their length) that grow in stable orbital zones around stars. Drift kelp is not dangerous, but it's a navigation hazard. Tendrils can be kilometers long, and a ship that flies through a kelp bed comes out trailing organic residue that gums up sensors and heat exchangers.

Keth harvest drift kelp as a raw material for bio-growth. Orryn consider it sacred (the only other living thing that drifts like they do). Compact navigation charts mark kelp beds as hazards. Sable Reach pirates hide in dense kelp beds because sensors can't see through them.

**What this looks like in art:**
- Ship emerging from a kelp bed, hull trailing long green-amber organic tendrils, crew on the exterior with cleaning equipment
- A Keth harvester vessel surrounded by drift kelp, arms of the ship extended to collect material
- A Sable Reach ambush: pirate ship barely visible through dense kelp, only engine glow betraying its position

### Station Rats (Faction-Specific)

Every faction has its own vermin, adapted to its environment:
- **Compact:** Actual rats. Descended from Earth stock. Maintenance hates them.
- **Keth:** Chitin-mites — small scavengers that eat shed exoskeleton fragments. Tolerated. They clean up.
- **Veshan:** Forge-beetles — heat-adapted insects that nest near forges and eat metal scale. Considered good luck by smiths.
- **Orryn:** Membrane-worms — translucent organisms that eat old membrane material. Sometimes chew through active walls.
- **Sable Reach:** Everything. Whatever survived the trip. The Reach ecosystem is a horror show.

## Smuggler Cant

The underground economy operates across species boundaries using **smuggler cant** — a pidgin that deliberately exploits the communication gaps between species to hide meaning from authorities.

Smuggler cant encodes trade information in species-specific channels: a price embedded in the rhythm of Veshan tail-flicks that humans can't read, a cargo description hidden in Orryn chromatophore patterns that Keth antennae can't detect, a meeting location transmitted through Keth pheromone trails that other species can't smell.

The genius is that each piece of information is partial. No single species can decode the whole message. You need a multi-species crew to assemble the full picture — which means the information is naturally secure from any single-species authority.

**What this looks like in art:**
- A smuggler's den: mixed-species group around a table, the human watching a Veshan's tail while the Orryn projects a holographic map that shifts in ways the Keth customs officer standing nearby can't interpret
- A pheromone dead-drop: a Keth casually touching a wall in a crowded market, leaving a chemical message that only the right nose will find
